### **Our Mission & Vision**

We do a lot of silly things at camp, but the underlying reason we are here is serious business. 

**Mission Statement:** The mission of Scouting America is to prepare young people to make ethical and moral choices over their lifetimes by instilling in them the values of the Scout Oath and Law. 

**Vision Statement:** Scouting America will prepare every eligible youth in America to become a responsible, participating citizen and leader who is guided by the Scout Oath and Law.

Everything we do is guided by the **Scout Oath** *(Duty to God and country, duty to others, duty to self)* and the **Scout Law** *(A Scout is Trustworthy, Loyal, Helpful, Friendly, Courteous, Kind, Obedient, Cheerful, Thrifty, Brave, Clean, and Reverent).* If your actions align with these principles, you're on the right track.

### **The Core Pillars of Summer Camp** 

An organized camping program like ours is an educational powerhouse that targets growth in four specific areas:

* **Physical:** Camp helps develop habits of healthy living through balanced meals and supervised physical activities.  
* **Mental:** Advancement programs offer challenges that build self-sufficiency and responsibility.  
* **Social:** The community of a summer camp helps build strong, morally rich people skills.  
* **Spiritual:** Spiritually rich time in nature helps develop a deeper understanding of a scout’s place in the universe.

### **The Chain of Command**

The chain of command is just a flowchart of who answers to who. 

* The **Camp Director** is the prime authority in camp.   
* Under the Camp Director is the **Program Director** and the structural core of camp: the **Ranger**, **Trading Post Manager/Business Manager**, **Medic**, **Kitchen Staff**, and **Camp Commissioner**.   
* All of the Program Staff (statistically, that’ll probably be you) fall under the Program Director.   
* Reporting directly to the Program Director are the **Area Directors**.   
* Reporting to the Area Directors are the rest of the **Program Staff (Instructors, Junior Staff, CITs)**. 

*![][image2]*

Put in a more practical way, there are two branches of the camp structure that matter to you:

1. The **Camp Director** runs the camp, making sure things are done safely, efficiently, on budget, and according to national standards. It’s their job to make sure everyone gets fed, the facilities are kept maintained, no one dies, and we don’t get shut down.   
2. The **Program Director** is in charge of the camp experience \- the merit badge and advancement classes, campfires, Funday, and the general camp staff. 

If you like, the Program Director handles everything “on stage” and the Camp Director handles everything “back stage.” 

Does that mean you don't have to do anything the Ranger or Commissioner asks you to do? Absolutely not. If they're asking you to do something, there's probably a reason. Generally speaking, unless it directly conflicts with your specific duties or your personal ethics or morality or if it’s illegal, go ahead and do it. If it does conflict, talk to your immediate director to figure out what should take priority. Don’t say no to anything without a good reason. 

**It should go without saying, health and safety emergencies will *always* take priority over any other duty.**

Also, as we’ll say repeatedly throughout this document, we have an open door policy. ANY issue, concern, grievance, or personal concerns can and should be brought to the administration and we will do our best to address it immediately. If you feel the need, you are also welcome to take your concerns directly to Catalina Council or the Council Executive Board. 

####  **Age Requirements for Staff Leadership** 

To comply with National Camp Accreditation Program (NCAP) and state labor guidelines, certain roles carry strict minimum age requirements.

* **Camp Director & Program Director:** Minimum age 21  
* **Range Activities Director and Climbing Director:** Minimum age 21\.   
* **Area Directors:** Minimum age 18  
* **Instructors / Junior Staff:** Minimum age 16  
* **Counselors in Training (CIT):** Ages 14-15.  

**CITs**  
Counselors in Training are in a unique position. They are considered part of a training program, kind of like interns. Labor laws limit the time 14 and 15 year olds can work (7am to 9pm and limited to 8 hours). It is everyone's job to make sure they are treated right. Not as “children”, but not as “employees.” They are our next generation of staff. They're not go-fers (“go for this, go for that”) or clean up servants, but **nor should they be left to teach a merit badge by themselves.** 

**Counselors-in-Training (CIT) and Volunteers**  
You will also see volunteer staff and CITs joining us for short-term rotations throughout the summer. These individuals are staff members, full stop. They are to be treated with the same professionalism and respect as any other colleague. To our campers and visitors, the distinction between a volunteer and a full-season staffer should be *invisible*.  
The CIT program provides an excellent way for future leaders to explore different departments and for camp leadership to identify where their talents shine brightest. By the end of their stay, CITs walk away with a deep appreciation for the effort required to keep the mountain running. They are incredibly valuable to our mission today and represent the future of our staff legacy.

**Junior Staff**  
As older teenagers, this qualifies as a summer job. Lower paying than the alternatives, but far more fulfilling. Studies have found that summer camp work is a far better preparation for excelling at any workforce than those who just worked retail or food service. They learn interpersonal skills, teaching, food service, cleaning, professional behavior, procedural adherence, how to work through the toughest conditions… The list goes on. To hiring managers who know, a history of camp staff is a gold mine for new hires. 

**Adult Staff**  
It may surprise you to learn that Adult Staff are anyone 18 and older. Most are likely to be Area Directors, but not all. Safeguarding Youth guidelines apply, more on that in Part 2, but otherwise there should be no difference between them and Junior Staff, besides hopefully a bit more maturity and leadership skill. Adult staff often consider themselves exempt from staff activities like running logs, campfires, and meal and shower cleanup. That is not the case. However, age brings with it some privileges and some limitations. Adults are more likely to be allowed to opt out of activities, be it a strong dislike of the activity or their age interferes with their involvement. As soon as you hit 30, parts of your body tend to start sabotaging you, and it gets worse every year. Running around leading 20 minutes of rousing camp songs is not always in the cards. That said, it is not fair or appropriate to ask the youth staff to do work the adults won't do. That of course comes with the caveat that the administration, medic, kitchen staff, ranger, Trading Post, commissioner, and area directors likely have their own tasks that take priority. Coffee is not *necessarily* one of them. 

**Support Staff**  
While they might not spend their day on "center stage" teaching merit badges, many essential departments keep the camp running behind the scenes. This includes the **kitchen**, **maintenance**, **Commissioners**, **Health Lodge**, **Trading Post**, and **Headquarters**. Make no mistake: these departments are every bit as vital as the program areas. Without these dedicated staff members, Lawton simply would not function. These support services follow the same leadership structure, each led by an Area Director and supported by their own assistants.  
Support staff work incredibly hard, often in high-pressure situations, to ensure the needs of every Scout and fellow staffer are met. They deserve the exact same level of *respect and courtesy* as the program team. We are one family, and every single person is a critical piece of the puzzle.

### **What are my duties anyway?**

I'm so glad you asked\! Camp Staff is much more than a regular job. So much so that calling it a “job” is woefully insufficient. It's a lifestyle. A calling. In the coming weeks, you will make friends, maybe enemies, probably a few frienemies. Heck, you might even fall in love (*see the section on Fraternization*)\! You'll do things you've never done and things you never thought you *could* do. You'll do tedious stuff, gross stuff, grueling stuff and painful stuff. It'll be really hard at times. But I promise, you can do it and it’ll be worth it. Afterward.  

#### **Here is a general list of what we expect of you (unless special arrangements are made with the Camp Director):**

* *Attend* all staff meetings, trainings, and Staff Week.   
* *Engage* in these meetings and trainings, offering your ideas for the continued improvement of camp. All ideas are valuable, no matter where you are on the chain.  
* Be *early* to things, not just on time. The classic rule of thumb is “if you’re not five minutes early, you’re late.”  
* *Participate* in and assist with all camp-wide programs such as flags, meals, campfires, and games.   
* Be *courteous* and respectful to everyone, especially campers and visitors.   
* *Follow* the chain of command. Your Area Director is your supervisor and your point person for any concerns. Area Directors will then go to the Program Director who then speaks to the Camp Director. That’s not to say, “you should never dare speak to the mysterious and god-like camp administration,” but a clear communication structure helps ensure the right information flows through the right channels.   
* Keep your appearance *presentable and respectable*. Shave, brush your hair, shower regularly, wear deodorant, brush your teeth, change your clothes on a regular basis (you probably won’t have 7 days worth of Class A and B uniforms to change into every day, but have backup options in case one gets dirty), and get haircuts on the weekends if needed and possible. Put simply, don’t look like a slob and for the sake of your cabin-mates, try not to stink.   
* Be in appropriate *uniform* for all activities. *See the Glossary section below for the uniform standards.* That means Class A/Field uniform for Flags, campfires, chapel services, and other formal activities. Class B/Activity uniform for daily program. “Civvies” (casual wear) can be worn on Staff Hill out of sight of campers, but all clothing must be scout appropriate (we’re not prudes, but if your grandmother would question your choices, it may not be the best choice for camp). Keep in mind that you may have to change again on short notice should something come up. Your down-time cannot always be guaranteed. If you are off of Staff Hill and not in at least Class B, you will be asked to go change immediately.   
* Maintain your *readiness and willingness* to perform your duties by eating properly and getting adequate sleep. This may sound like something that should go without saying, but it's the most common problem we have with staff members (including the admin team).   
* Keep your *staff quarters* clean, orderly, and comfortable for all residents. We do not plan to have cabin inspections unless it becomes necessary. Don’t make it necessary.  
* Take proper care of all camp equipment and facilities.  
* Be mindful that the primary responsibility of each staff member is to *be of service* to the Scouts, camp, and each other.   
* Take *personal responsibility* for the success of the entire camp. Even if it’s not in your job description, there’s always something you can do to make it better.   
* *Name Tags*, indicating that you are Camp Lawton 2026 Staff,  must be visible at all times. It should be on the top half of your body, either hung on a lanyard around your neck or clipped to your shirt.   
* Abide by the Scout Oath, Law, and ALL of the ideals of scouting.

**Other duties as assigned…** The list above is by no means a comprehensive list of what your duties will be over the summer. Covering everything would make this a much longer book and would still likely miss some of the more unpredictable possibilities. Just be prepared to do whatever it takes to make this summer at Camp Lawton a success.

#### **The Junior Staff Conundrum**

Under ideal conditions, the vast majority of Camp Lawton's staff is made up of Junior Staff and CITs between 14 and 17\. That of course presents this chilling challenge: we've got a bunch of hormonal mixed gender teenagers running this place\! How are we ever to survive? 

Later on, we'll have a Code of Conduct for you to sign with all the official rules for you to follow, but let's just address a few things with the blunt honest maturity I trust you can handle.

**Phones**

I'm not going to be one of those people who blames all of society's woes on kids who won't look up from their smartphones. You've got lives on there. I get it. However, Camp Lawton needs you *here* if we're going to get what we need from you, and if you're going to get anything out of the summer yourself. Keep it put away. That goes for tablets and laptops as well (unless they’re approved for use as a program aid). We will begin the summer with no blanket ban on phones, but any abuse and we will have to drop the hammer. 

**Photography and social media**

While we’re at it, we encourage taking photographs and videos of camp. Ideally, use a dedicated camera device so we don’t run into any phone abuse issues. However, the sharing of scouts’ faces on social media can get really messy. Run any content you intend to post to social media by the Camp Director. It’s not censorship, we just don’t want to put any kids in harm’s way. 

**Video Games and other recreation**

Camp can be stressful and we all need a healthy release. In your limited off time, video games can be great for that. So can books, music, movies, TV, even card and board games. If you want to start up a D\&D campaign, I might even join you\! 

**However**… we're gonna have a problem if: 

1. You're too loud and are heard by scouts or leaders. It doesn't take much.  
2. You are late to or miss a program because of it.   
3. You are playing, watching, or doing something not in line with scout values. That means M rated games, R rated movies, card games with excessive language, violence or sexual content, etc. Cards Against Humanity is fun, but stick to Apples To Apples.

And leave your huge gaming rigs at home\! Yes, I’ve seen it, and it’s embarrassing. 

**Drugs, Alcohol, Pornography** 

**No, no, and no.** Duh. If you don't know why, this may not be the right place for you. Yes, prescription drugs are fine as long as they're registered with the medic. No, I'm not going to debate with you what qualifies as pornography. And don't give me any “but weed isn't an addictive drug” or “it's non-alcoholic beer.” This is a Scout camp\! Binge on Mountain Dew and sour gummy worms like the rest of us\!

**Fraternization** 

We’ll be getting to SYT guidelines in Part 2, but let’s talk about something more inevitable. Over the summer, you’ll develop close bonds with your fellow staff. Sometimes *very* close. I've been there myself. Whatever your “trapped together/coming-of-age YA romance” love story may be, keep the physical stuff out of camp. Why? 

1. **Morality\!** You're Scouts. On staff, you have the responsibility of representing the pinnacle of scouting values, which don’t include PDA.    	  
2. **The Birds and the Bees\!** Holding hands leads to kissing. Kissing leads to French kissing. Yadda-yadda-yadda. And that's how babies are made. Let's not please.   
3. **Drama\!** Sure, smooching is fun\! But you're stuck here for the season with these people. If something goes wrong, now we've *all* got to suffer.   
4. **Professionalism\!** Fraternization can put us on the path to harassment, abuse, or assault complaints. That means people getting fired, lawsuits, civil or criminal trials, and entirely too much *paperwork*. Trust me, no one wants *any of that* and you getting handsy at camp just isn't worth it. 

Sound fair? To be clear, I wouldn’t dream of getting in the way of your personal awakenings, but as you’ll learn in the Safeguarding Youth Training, there is zero tolerance for some of this stuff and I want to make sure you’re safe from any of that.

### **Stress Management and Mental Stability**

*“At some point, everything’s gonna go south and you’re going to say, this is it. This is how I end. Now, you can either accept that, or you can get to work. That’s all it is. You just begin. You do the math. You solve one problem and you solve the next one and then the next. And if you solve enough problems, you get to come home.” \- The Martian (2015), screenwriter Drew Goddard*

Let's be honest: staffing is HARD. Feeling stressed, overwhelmed, cranky, or even a little depressed at camp doesn't mean you are failing. It means your brain is reacting normally to a highly unnatural, high-stimulus environment. You are being tasked with managing the safety, education, and entertainment of dozens of younger kids with very little time for yourself. That carries a massive cognitive load. We ask a lot of you, but not *that*.  

Here is how you survive the summer with your sanity intact:

**1\. Work the Problem**  
When the stress hits, it’s easy to spiral into catastrophic thinking (*"I can't do this," "Everyone hates me," "This summer is awful"*). Stop and work the problem. Run a self-diagnostic:

* *Are you dehydrated?* Dehydration mimics anxiety, spiking your heart rate and making you irritable. Remember your Water Appreciation Moments (WAM).  
* *Are you fueled?* Surviving solely on energy drinks and sour gummy worms will crash your system. Eat actual food at the Dining Hall.  
* *Are you sleep-deprived?* Getting enough sleep can be tricky at camp when you’ve got a lot on your mind and with a cabin full of other people with a lot on theirs. Lights Out isn’t just an arbitrary rule. It’s there to give you ample opportunity to get the sleep you need. Use that time from 10pm to 6am for *sleeping*\!  

**2\. Use Your Siesta** Science has consistently confirmed that napping for a short time each day improves mental health and productivity. The daily schedule intentionally builds in a "Siesta" gap between lunch and the afternoon program. It is tempting to use that time for all the stuff you've been meaning to get done, but it is literally a “nap time” set aside as time for staff, scouts, and leaders to reboot. Go to your cabin, lie down, and let your brain stop processing external input for an hour. You don't have to sleep, but you do need to relax.  
**3\. Manage Sensory Overload** Camp is loud. Between the incessant chatter of preteen kids, the cheering on The Logs, and living in a cabin with fellow staff, you rarely experience true silence. If you feel yourself getting overstimulated, find a quiet space out of sight of the campers where you can safely decompress. Listen to the breeze in the trees. Give your nervous system a chance to return to baseline. Just remember your Area Director needs to know where you are and if you're under 18, you do still need a buddy. Duct tape is available if your buddy needs some encouragement to *silently* enjoy the gentle sounds of the forest. (For legal reasons, that’s a joke.)  
**4\. Tag Out (The Strategic Retreat)** If a scout is pushing your buttons, a lesson is completely derailing, or you feel a meltdown coming on, do not force yourself to power through until you snap. Tell your Area Director, "I need five minutes." Going somewhere “away” and taking a few deep breaths and resetting is a hallmark of professional competence, not a sign of weakness.  
**5\. Call in the Grown-Ups** If your mental health is slipping, if you are experiencing severe homesickness, or if a situation with a camper or cabinmate is too heavy for you to carry alone, go to an adult staff member that you trust. You are *never* expected to go through *anything* alone. Please don’t suffer in silence.  

**A reality check**  
As dangerous as it is to get overworked and burned out, it can also be all too easy to say “the Staff Handbook says I should prioritize my mental health, so I'm going to just stay in bed all week.” It happens to even the strongest of us and it's easy to spiral into a lazy depression. As miserable as you may be, don't give up on your job. Talk to people. Cling to your purpose. Hiding from your responsibilities is *never* going to be the answer. Believe me, it just makes you feel worse. If you really need some time off, talk to the administration. We’ll work something out. 

### **Glossary**

**Class A / Field Uniform:** 

* An official Scout uniform shirt \- this can be Scouts BSA, Explorer, Venture, Sea Scouts, or an international Scouting organization you’re registered with  
* Scout pants/shorts \- NOT jeans\! They must be official uniform bottoms (Scouts BSA is olive green for instance, Venture is a grey). Officially licenced skirts or other types of leg coverings are also acceptable. As the weather varies in camp, both shorts and pants are advisable.   
* Belt \- A scouting approved belt, whether an officially branded belt or a leather belt with a scouting related belt buckle, as long as it’s appropriate and indicates scouting in some way.   
* Closed toed shoes \- We recommend hiking boots or reliable sneakers that will hold up to significant abuse on the rough trails of camp. Closed toed shoes must be worn at all times in camp unless in your cabin or taking a shower (in which case we advise water shoes, flip flops, or crocs to prevent fungal outbreaks).  
* Socks \- We will not be picky about official Scout socks, but you should be wearing socks of some kind.    
* An undershirt \- While optional, the varying weather of the mountain makes layering our friend. The easiest undershirt is your staff shirt, making it easy to switch between Class A and Class B.    
* Hat (optional) \- if worn with the Class A, it must be scout related in some way.

**Class B / Activity Uniform:**

* Camp Lawton Staff Shirt \- Primarily this year’s staff shirt, but past year staff shirts are acceptable. If necessary, any Camp Lawton shirt may be worn with administration approval. Other scouting related shirts may be approved by the Camp or Program Director.   
* Scout pants/shorts \- See above.  
* Closed toed shoes \- See above.  
* Socks \- See above.   
* Hat (optional) \- Must be camp appropriate, but keeping the sun off your head and neck is an excellent idea.  
* Water Bottle \- It’s not enough to rely on drinking fountains around camp. You must have a water container on you at all times. *This is part of your uniform.* 

**The Logs:** The bleacher seating in front of the Dining Hall was, for a century, made of logs. Though they have received a substantial upgrade in favor of aesthetics and comfort, we still call them The Logs out of tradition. This is where scouts will gather for meals and staff will lead songs and activities while they wait. 

**WAM:** Water Appreciation Moment. If heard, everyone within earshot must take a drink of water. This is because dehydration is a very real problem in camp and drinking water should be aggressively encouraged among both staff and scouts. 

**Troop Friend:** Youth staff will be assigned to act as Troop Friends for assigned troops. The Troop Friend will act as the unit’s guide on Sunday, a liaison and friend of the troop throughout the week, and inspect the campsite before the unit leaves on Sunday.  You will have the opportunity to make friends, inspire young scouts, and perhaps guide Scouts in the direction of becoming camp staff themselves. Troop Friends are not expected to spend all their spare time with their troop; just make an appearance now and then. It makes a bigger difference than you think.  

**Staff Hill:** The hill on the north end of camp designated for Staff living facilities.  

**Smellables:** Anything with an odor or scent that might attract wildlife. All Smellables must be secured in the Smellables Shed by the youth shower house.

### **Customer Service**

One of the most important parts of your job on camp staff is making scouts feel appreciated and encouraging an atmosphere where scouts can thrive. In fact, Camp Lawton’s most powerful asset is its size and staff. Ask any scout who has gone to a larger camp to name five staff members. They won’t be able to. That’s because at other camps, staff are just there to deliver the program. A camp our size allows us to build personal relationships with the scouts and give them unique experiences they won’t get anywhere else. To help you understand your role, here’s a few conceptual methods to follow. Much of it is directed more toward management, but it’s helpful for you to understand it as well.  

#### **Dennis Snow’s customer service tips from Disney**

1. **Hire for Attitude:** Instead of hiring based solely on skills and trying to train personality later, prioritize hiring naturally friendly people and then train them on the necessary skills.  
2. **Everything Speaks:** Every detail, from the cleanliness of your facility to how quickly you respond to a request, sends a message to your scouts and their leaders about how much you value them. You must own every aspect of the experience.  
3. **It's a Stage:** Treat every employee as a cast member and every job as a performance. Maintain a clear distinction between the "onstage" experience for the customer and the "backstage" operations that should never be visible to them.  
4. **Map First:** Before training, map out every touchpoint through the lens of the customer. Identifying these points allows you to prevent service breakdowns before they occur.  
5. **Prioritize Systems over Scripts:** Excellence should be built into repeatable, reliable systems that anyone can follow, rather than relying on individual heroics or rigid, unnatural scripts.

#### **FISH Philosophy** 

1. **Play**: Bring a lighthearted, spontaneous energy to your environment. This isn't about ignoring your responsibilities or enforcing “mandatory workplace fun.” It’s about finding creativity and joy in routine tasks. The work still gets done, but the atmosphere feels less rigid.  
2. **Make Their Day**: Look for ways to create a memorable, positive experience for someone else. It doesn’t require massive grand gestures or giving away free products. It is usually a small, unexpected interaction \- remembering a name, sharing a genuine laugh, or sincerely validating a customer's frustration.  
3. **Be There**: Give the customer (or your coworkers) your complete, undivided attention. This is often the hardest step to maintain. It means putting away distractions, pausing your mental to-do list, and actively listening. You are treating the person in front of you as the only thing that matters at that moment.   
4. **Choose Your Attitude**: Take total responsibility for the mindset you bring to work each day.You can't control angry customers, bad weather, or system outages. But you can decide how you react to them. It is an active, daily choice you make before you walk through the door, not a permanent personality trait.

#### **Teaching With E.D.G.E.**

The official method Scouting America teaches everyone is the EDGE method. It’s a solid method.   
Edge stands for:

* **Explain**: Tell them what you’re gonna tell them.  
* **Demonstrate**: Show them how to do it.  
* **Guide**: Walk them through it, letting them try it a couple times.  
* **Enable**: Test and give them the tools to go on to take the skill with them into the future.

 

#### **Game Design Method**

That’s awesome and all, but consider this…  
The best video games have a very specific method to give players an enjoyable way to learn to play a game. It’s similar to EDGE, but a bit more fun. Let’s use Level 1-1 of the original Super Mario Bros. to illustrate. 

1. **Explain**: Oh no\! Princess Toadstool has been kidnapped by the evil King Koopa\! Let’s go save her\!  
2. **Demonstrate**: The first thing you see in the level is a single slow moving Goomba, conveniently shaped like an arrow as if to imply “you should jump here.” After you’ve figured out how to target a jump, you’re rewarded with points, some floating bricks to break and some “?” block containing a power up and coins.   
3. **Guide**: As you go forward, you are shown a few new elements of the gameplay you’ll need to maneuver including pipes. One pipe you can go down for a bunch of free coins. If you choose to skip the pipe, there’s a hidden one up. Then you face your first first gap to jump. After the gap, things start ramping up. You get a second tier power-up (the fire flower) if you can survive the Goomba’s dropping down from the platform above. Then you meet your first Koopa Troopa that introduces a new game mechanic of the sliding shell.  
4. **Enable**: From there, things get more complicated and you will need to use all of the skills you’ve learned to complete the level. By the time you reach the flag, you are fully prepared to take on level 1-2. 

So how can that possibly relate to teaching an 11 year old scout how to tie a square knot? Well try this out. 

1. **Explain**: Oh no\! You need to tie down a parcel\! Let’s tie a square knot\!   
2. **Demonstrate**: You introduce the concept of a working end and a standing end of the rope. The working end is the one that does the work, the standing end just kind of plays along. Then show them the basic overhand, leading with the working end. Then another overhand, again leading with the working end.   
3. **Guide**: So now that they know the basics, they can try it out themselves. You can play around a bit with the terminology. Some people get the “working end vs standing end” method better while some people find the “right over left, left over right” easier to understand. Everyone learns a little differently so you want to experiment a bit to see what works for your students. Get them doing it and be on the move making each person feel comfortable. With knots, often handling the anxiety is most of the battle. It’s kinda like math.   
4. **Enable**: Now we actually try tying some stuff down with it. Shake things up, try out different variations on the premise of the square knot and challenge them. Did you know if you yank one end of a Granny Knot you get Two Half Hitches? Did you know you could tie a Square Knot with a bite around another byte? Did you know if you do that method differently, you can create a Thieves' Knot? The Square knot isn’t even its real name. It’s a Reef Knot, and the Ashley Book of Knots says it’s one of the most useless knots out there for anything actually important. Once you’ve shown them the amazing world of everything the Square Knot has to offer, not only are they going to walk away fully understanding the Square Knot, they now *like* knots and are primed to learn the next knots\!

I just think that’s a lot more fun than the dry “tell them what you’re going to tell them, tell them, then tell them what you told them” mantra in teaching.

### **Communication: Darmok and Jalad at Tanagra**

When we are getting ready for a new summer, we have the monumental task of bringing together hardened veterans, green newbies, and young CITs who have never done a chore, let alone held a job (not you of course. You’re great\!). 

The trick is, the veterans already know Lawton. Know how to call someone on the radio, know that when we say “the logs” we mean those benches over there that aren’t logs at all. We speak in a shorthand that makes perfect sense to us but to outsiders, it’s as baffling as an alien language.   
   
Which brings us to something very special (and very nerdy). In the classic *Star Trek: The Next Generation* episode, “Darmok,” the Enterprise has been assigned to try to establish diplomatic relations with an enigmatic alien race known as Tamarians. The Universal Translator is working, but giving only what seem to be nonsense proper nouns. Through a series of trials, Picard learns that Dathon, the Tamarian captain, “speaks in metaphor.” “Darmok and Jalad at Tanagra” is about two hunters who learned to understand each other, fought a mighty beast, and left together as friends. “Shaka when the walls fell” is failure, sadness, or simply “no.”

Here is how we’ll apply that lesson to survive Staff Week, connect with our campers, and keep from losing our minds.

#### **Cutting Through the Alphabet Soup**

When you rattle off camp lingo, you’re speaking Tamarian. If a senior staffer says, “Go grab the Ranger from the Ogre, stop by STEM in the Commissioner’s Shack and pick up their SOP and bring them to the EMT at the Logs,” a new staffer will stare at you like you’re loony. 

* **Be a Cultural Guide:** If you’ve been here before, it’s your responsibility to translate our traditions and look out for the new folks.  
* **Explain the “Why”:** Explain what you’re talking about. *I’m* not even sure why the Ranger’s tool shed is called The Black Ogre, but that’s what it’s called. The STEM program runs out of what was once the Commissioner’s Shack, and it still clings to that name decades after the last time a Camp Commissioner ever stayed in that cabin. An SOP is a Standard Operating Procedure. EMT is an Emergency Medical Technician, a medical certification our Medic may have. The Logs I explained earlier as the bleachers in front of the Dining Hall.    
* **Reveal the Hidden Map:** Camp has a "hidden curriculum"—unspoken rules about where you can sneak some cell signal, what time you can catch the showers when no one is around, what compliments work to tell the Program Director to get out of cleaning the KYBO behind the youth shower house (I’m kidding, that wont work… chocolate though…). Share those secrets freely. Bring the newbies into the cool kid club, don’t gatekeep.

#### **The El-Adrel Principle (The Power of Shared Toil)**

In the episode, Picard and Dathon finally bond while marooned on El-Adrel Four, fighting for survival. By facing a physical trial together, they bypass words and build a shared understanding. 

**Staff Week** is our El-Adrel. Shared labor is a huge equalizer and teams bond best when faced with a common task. Collective physical struggle builds nonverbal coordination and group trust fast. The more you work with different people and the harder the work is, the closer your bond will be, leading to better chemistry through the summer and more productivity all around. 

#### **Assuming Positive Intent (Dealing with Friction)**

The long days of camp can be hard sometimes. You’re going to get frustrated, maybe even get angry and lash out. *“Zinda, his face black, his eyes red”* our Tamarian friends would say.

When communication fails here, small gripes can turn into massive headaches. In the episode, Dathon throws a dagger at Picard. A "**low-context**" response would be to see it as an attack. Picard, however, realized Dathon was offering a tool for their mutual survival.

You have to apply that same grace to your coworkers and campers:

* **Look at the Surroundings:** If someone snaps at you, there’s probably a reason for it. Maybe they’re frustrated about something else, maybe something you did or said hit a nerve you didn’t know, or maybe they just have a headache. .   
* **Truly Listen:** When someone is acting out, look for the root cause rather than getting defensive. Talk to them privately and hear what they’re actually saying.  
* **Speak from the “I”:** Use "I statements" (like, "When this happens, I feel...") to find solutions together instead of starting a fight. Psychologists recommend this method for a reason. It avoids sounding accusatory and takes responsibility for your side of the situation.

#### **Reading the Air**

Memorizing this handbook and all the safety rules won't save you when a camper is in an emotional tailspin. Working with youth requires empathy and the ability to sense what’s unsaid. 

* **Identify the Cues:** If a scout is hiding from their troop, they aren’t just being stubborn. They are likely *“Darmok on the ocean,”* or feeling isolated, homesick, or scared.  
* **Lead with Heart:** Use the **U-S-A model**: **Understand** why they’ve pulled away, **Speak** with them one-on-one, and **Act** to get them the help they need.

#### **So what does that mean?**

It basically means skits and songs performed in camp cannot contain anything that could possibly make anyone feel bad. Is it a bit heavy handed? Probably. That doesn’t make it any less important. The important thing to remember is that **camp is meant to be a positive, safe space for *everyone***. That includes everyone from disabled or special needs people to people who hate the word “moist.” They all deserve to have a good experience without feeling mocked. 

This probably knocks out a lot of the skits and songs you already know. 

But that's ok\! Creativity thrives in adversity. Most of the skits and songs you know are probably over 50 years old anyway\! Let's make up some new stuff\!

Making campers laugh is one of the best parts of working on staff, but it takes a little finesse to pull off a campfire that is genuinely funny, memorable, and appropriate. Here is an advanced guide for writing and performing solid comedy, pulling the best advice from professional writers and performers.

#### 

#### **Performance Fundamentals**

Before we get too deep, here are the basic **natural laws** of performing in a campfire.

* E-nun-ci-ate \- You can’t tell a joke if the audience can’t hear or understand you. Speak clearly, deliberately, and a little bit louder than you think feels necessary.   
* Pacing \- Even the funniest joke gets boring if it overstays its welcome. Pacing is more an art than a science. This is why rehearsal is extremely helpful.   
* Rule of 3 \- It’s one of the oldest rules on comedy for a reason. One beat to establish the scene, one beat to set up the joke, and one beat for the punchline.   
1. A duck walks up to a lemonade stand and asks for grapes, gets a no, and waddles away.   
2. The duck walks up again and the angry clerk threatens to nail his bill to the floor if he asks again.   
3. The duck walks up again and asks for *nails*. Upon getting a confused “no”, he asks for grapes.   
   There are ways to get more mileage out of a joke if you and the audience are really enjoying it, but in those cases there needs to be a proper story arc so that you’re not just repeating the same gag ad nauseum. Again, rehearse.   
* Read the audience \- You’re not up there in a vacuum. You’ve got an audience you’re trying to entertain. Take a moment to pay attention to them. How are they feeling? Is your song too long? Are your skits too rushed? Is it cold out? Are there bugs making them miserable? All of these things should be accounted for.   
* Don’t sweat the mistakes \- The biggest laughs you’re likely to get are bound to be when you screw something up. That’s ok. Embrace it. Make it part of the skit. Carefully following a *script* will lead to madness. More complicated skits often need a script to hit all the right beats, but good reliable skits are built off of improvisation and personality.   
* Enthusiasm, energy, absurdity \- The fact of the matter is, if you leave that campfire stage with any dignity left, you did it wrong. 

#### **Leading Songs**

**How do you start a song?**   
Do you say, “who wants to sing Tootie Ta?” NO\!\!\! Do you ask “what do you want to sing?” They’ll answer with everything from “Nothing\!” to the latest wildly inappropriate pop song. 

The best way to start a song is to START DOING IT\! Just run up to the crowd and yell as loud as you can, “THIS IS A REPEAT AFTER ME SONG\!” Repeat songs are the easiest way to get started because they pretty much force participation. After you’ve got them going, you can go with the more casual singalongs. Enthusiasm is everything. If you’re having a blast out there, they’ll follow. 

**But why do we even sing songs? No one really likes it\!**  
They say they don’t. I don’t always. Just let me sit and suffer through my morning in peace and quiet until I can eat my food. It’s a fair feeling. The reason we do it is because songs bring people together. It’s a psychological thing. When you’re out there sticking out your buns during Tootie-Ta, you’re part of something. Something silly, but it means something very real deep down. There’s a reason churches (and, admittedly, cults) sing hymns and repeat ritualistic phrases. It opens them up to the communal spirit and makes them more open to embracing what we have to offer them. Scouts who spent a week singing songs and yelling in unison will leave camp feeling like they had a much more powerful experience than those who just sat on the logs waiting for their food. What makes us not a cult, exactly? Well, we usually serve generic brand lemonade, not Kool-aid. 

#### **The Campfire Arc** 

A campfire should have an arc not entirely unlike the fire itself. It should start off with a bang, something active, exciting, rousing. Then put some of your best stuff up front. You can fit in the more mundane stuff in the middle, then "throw another log on the fire" with more of your best stuff as the climax. Then one or two slower but still memorable bits (a meaningful song or story) before your inspirational ending.

In the case of a Staff Campfire, **don’t MC it**. The staff should know what they’re doing, you don’t need to introduce the next skit. Saturday Night Live doesn’t say “up next, the game show parody and on deck is Weekend Update.” Just keep the surprises coming. 

When it comes to the Camper Campfire, we usually have one or two SPL’s do the job, and they’ll need to introduce the next troop and who’s on deck. Don’t say the name of the skit, just in case the name gives away the punchline. Just the troop. Remember, the Program Director MUST approve all campfire content.

### **Severe Weather Preparedness**

**Training Requirements & Compliance**

* **Mandatory for Staff:** Under National Camp Accreditation Program (NCAP) Standard SQ-402, **all camp staff must complete the Hazardous Weather Training online before youth participants arrive**. It is also required for all registered Direct Contact Leaders (e.g., Scoutmasters, Cubmasters).

* **Online Only:** The training takes about 30 minutes aynd **must be completed individually online** via My.Scouting.org. In-person or group-facilitated classes are strictly prohibited and will not be recorded in official training systems.

**Lightning Safety & The 30/30 Rule**

* **"When Thunder Roars, Go Indoors":** If you can hear thunder, lightning is a danger.  
* **The 30-Second Rule:** If the time between a lightning flash and the thunder is 30 seconds or less, the storm is within 6 miles. **Suspend all outdoor activities immediately** and seek shelter.  
* **The 30-Minute Rule:** Do not leave shelter or resume outdoor activities until **at least 30 minutes have passed since the last audible thunder or visible lightning**.  
* **Safe Shelters:** You must use a primary shelter (an enclosed building with a roof, walls, floor, and plumbing/wiring) or a secondary shelter (a fully enclosed hard-topped metal vehicle with rolled-up windows). **In our case, the primary lightning and storm shelter is the Dining Hall.**   
* **Tents, open pavilions, and golf carts offer zero protection from lightning**.   
* **Caught in the Open:** If you cannot reach shelter, avoid tall/isolated trees, water, and metal objects. Disperse the group so individuals are 15 to 100 feet apart, and do NOT lie flat on the ground.

**Heat & Thermal Stress Mitigation**

* **Acclimation & Hydration:** we are at almost 8,000 ft above sea level and more than 5,000 ft above Tucson. It takes a few days to properly adjust to the decreased air pressure. Be ready for that and prepare scouts for it. The larger issue at Camp Lawton is dehydration. The environment will dehydrate you quickly. You and the scouts should be drinking several liters of water a day. Don't force it, over-hydration is a thing too, but drink when you're thirsty. Alternate it with some electrolyte sports drink if you're having any trouble. If you're feeling a headache, nose bleed, dizzy, and/or fatigued, sit down and relax in the shade for a few minutes sipping your water. If it continues, get to the Health Lodge. Instruct scouts (and leaders) to do the same.   
* **Heat Exhaustion vs. Heatstroke:**  
  * *Heat Exhaustion* (pale/clammy skin, heavy sweating, nausea): Move to shade, remove excess clothing, cool with cold water, and hydrate. Do not give salt tablets.  
  * *Heatstroke* (altered mental state, confusion, extremely hot/flushed skin): **This is a life-threatening emergency.** Call EMS/911 immediately, initiate rapid cooling (ice packs, cold water immersion), and do not give anything by mouth.  
  * Camp Lawton is in the mountains. It rarely gets much higher than 90°, nothing like what we're used to in the desert, but it's still enough to get sick from the heat. Hypothermia is also not impossible in our environment. 

**High Winds & Hazard Trees**

* **Activity Suspension:** High winds or microbursts can cause extreme localized damage. Climbing must be suspended immediately if winds exceed predetermined camp limits. We also may shut down Range and Target Activities if the range master determines the wind is unsafe.  
* **Hazard Trees ("Widowmakers"):** Look out for dead trees, live trees with dead parts, or unstable structures. **Never set up tents, hammocks, or program areas within a hazard tree's fall radius.** The fall radius on flat ground is 1.5 times the total height of the tree. Management works with the Forest Service each year to bring down hazard trees, but thanks to fires, bark beetles, droughts, and storms, there are bound to still be some trees that may pose a threat. Be aware of these, stay clear, and watch for any leaning trees, signs of the trees swaying more than usual in the wind and listen for cracking sounds. Report these to the Camp Director.

**Camp Emergency Action Plans (EAP) & Alarms**

* **Property-Specific EAP:** Every camp has a specific, written Emergency Action Plan covering procedures for weather, fires, lost campers, and medical emergencies. All staff must be trained on this and have access to it.  
* **Camp Lawton’s EAP:** In the event of an emergency, the camp bell will begin ringing continuously. **EVERYONE must report directly to the parade grounds (the flags)**. There we'll do a head count, and proceed with further instructions. We'll give some more detail on this later. 

### **Safeguarding Youth**

Scouting is still recovering from the fallout of some very dark stories. It’s uncomfortable to talk about, but it must be addressed. The important thing to understand is that it’s not about the individual abusers here; it’s about the culture they create that allows the abuse. A scoutmaster doesn’t crawl into the sleeping bag of a youth if there are people around that are unwilling to allow it. Our commitment to Safeguarding Youth is not just about preventing abuse. **It’s about making Camp Lawton a place where *that* *could never happen*.**  

The following is a quick reference for the Safeguarding Youth Training content. We will be addressing it during staff training and you will need to complete the online course module annually. These are the most critical rules in the scouting program, and do not allow for any grey area. 

It should also be noted that some of the guidelines have changed since the Youth Protection days, so when making decisions, make sure to check that the policy you're remembering is SYT not YPT. 

**Safeguarding Youth Training (SYT) Requirements**

* **Annual Requirement:** All staff members and all registered adult scout leaders (18+) must complete the Safeguarding Youth Training annually.  
* **Course Structure:** The initial course takes 90 minutes. Subsequent annual renewals require a 15–20 minute refresher video and an exam, which must be passed with a score of 80% or higher.  
* **Age Transitions:** Camp staff members who turn 18 during the season *instantly* become subject to all adult rules, including separate housing and the prohibition of one-on-one contact with youth.

**Supervision and Contact**

* **Two-Deep Leadership:** A minimum of two registered adult leaders (21+) is required at all times for all activities. If female youth are present, at least one of the leaders must be a registered female adult.  
* **No One-on-One Contact:** One-on-one contact between adults (including staff 18+) and youth is strictly prohibited in person, online, via text, or on any digital platform.  
* **The Buddy System:** Youth must always use the buddy system. Buddy pairs must be single-gender and no more than two years apart in age. **Staff members or adults (18+) cannot be buddies with youth members**.

**Privacy and Accommodations**

* **Separate Accommodations:** Male and female youth, as well as youth and adults, must have completely separate tenting and lodging. **No adult or staff member over 18 may enter or sleep in youth lodging**.  
* **Restroom Privacy:** Separate shower and latrine facilities must be provided for adults and youth, and for males and females. Camp Lawton has designated adult and youth shower houses, and each has individual private facilities. Staff Hill also has individual private facilities with locks for showers and toilets.    
* **Respect of Privacy:** Adults may only intrude on youth privacy for health and safety emergencies. The use of cameras, smartphones, mirrors, or drones in areas where privacy is expected (like showers or changing areas) is strictly prohibited.

**Behavior and Discipline**

* **Constructive Discipline:** Discipline must always be constructive, positive, and reflect Scouting's values. Corporal punishment, isolation, humiliation, and ridicule are strictly prohibited.  
* **Zero Tolerance:** There is a strict zero-tolerance policy for hazing, initiations, secret organizations, bullying, cyberbullying, or physical violence.  
* **No Sexual Activity:** All sexual activity and inappropriate public displays of affection are strictly prohibited at camp.  
* **Appropriate Attire:** Modest and proper clothing is required at all times. For example, swimwear must be modest (e.g., no tight-fitting swim briefs or bikinis).

**Incident Response and Mandatory Reporting**

* **Intervene Immediately:** If you witness abuse, policy violations, or unsafe behavior, you must step in immediately to stop it. Do not dismiss inappropriate actions as "just kids being kids".  
* **Confidentiality:** Any information concerning campers is strictly confidential. This is of the utmost importance when dealing with suspected cases of child abuse. The camper must never be informed of this notification process. His/her knowledge of this report could endanger him/her when he/she returns home.   
* **The Perpetrator:** If an alleged perpetrator is on camp grounds, they will be immediately separated from the campers until the incident is resolved, until the threat is removed, or as long as necessary to protect the safety and welfare of the campers.   
* **Mandatory Reporters:** All camp staff and volunteers are mandated reporters. This duty cannot be delegated to anyone else.  
* **Reporting Chain:** You must immediately report any good-faith suspicion of abuse or policy violations to the Camp Director. At that point, *both of you* are responsible for contacting the necessary authorities. You will need to speak to DCS as described below while the Camp Director contacts the Scout Executive. The reason for this painful process is to prevent any chance or accusations of institutional concealment by the organization. Don't be afraid of this, you're doing the right thing.  
* **Scouts First Helpline:** Scouting America offers the **24-hour** **Scouts First Helpline at 1-844-SCOUTS1 (1-844-726-8871)** in the event you need immediate assistance regarding a youth safety concern and the Camp Director is unavailable.   
* **Immediate Risk:** Call 911 immediately if someone is at immediate risk of harm.

**The Law: Mandatory Reporting (ARS 13-3620)** As a camp staff member, **YOU** are a mandated reporter. This isn't just a Scouting policy; it is Arizona State Law (ARS 13-3620). YOU (not the Camp Director or Council Executive) are legally required to report all concerns of child abuse or neglect to The Arizona Department of Child Safety (DCS). Reach out to the 24/7 state hotline by calling 1-888-SOS-CHILD (888-767-2445). In a life-threatening crisis, skip the hotline and **dial 911** immediately. While *mandated reporters* can submit non-emergency details via the Guardian online portal, the simple reality is that every citizen is **legally required** to *report suspected abuse without delay* to the Department of Child Safety or the local authorities.

**Legal Definitions you need to know:**

1. **Child Abuse:** Harm or threatened harm to a child's health or welfare, which occurs through non-accidental physical or mental injury, sexual abuse, or maltreatment.  
2. **Child Neglect:** Harm to a child's health or welfare by a person responsible for them, which occurs through negligent treatment (failure to provide adequate food, clothing, shelter, emotional, or medical care).

### **Bear and Wildlife Safety**

Camp Lawton is located within active black bear habitat. While sightings are rare, it does not take much for a wildlife encounter to escalate. Strict adherence to camp policies is our most effective method for preventing these incidents. 

1. **Prevention: The "Smellables" Policy \-** No food, drink, or "smellables" (toiletries, deodorant, and other scented products) are allowed anywhere in camp except in the immediate vicinity of the Dining Hall. The only exception is plain water.  
   1. **Storage:** Overnight, all smellables must be secured in the "Bear Box" and brought to the Smellables Shed located next to the youth shower house.  
   2. **Enforcement:** If you see anyone with food or scented items outside of allowed areas, politely instruct them to relocate the items to the proper storage area, and report the incident to senior staff.  
2. **Protocol for Bear Sightings \-** If you see a bear in camp, remain calm. Black bears will usually ignore you or run off if you make your presence known and show yourself to be a human threat.  
   1. Immediately report the sighting to the Camp Director as a **Code Brown**.  
   2. If it is safe to do so, adult staff should maintain a distant, safe visual on the animal so camp administration can track its movements.  
   3. The Camp Director will handle contacting the Game and Fish Department.  
3. **In the Event of an Attack \-** In the highly unlikely event of an attack, the immediate priority is scaring the animal away from the victim.   
   1. **Scare the Bear:** Yell loudly and throw rocks, but **do not approach the bear**.   
   2. **Call for Help:** Immediately dispatch someone to locate the Camp Director and the Ranger.  
   3. **First Aid & Assembly:** As soon as the animal retreats, begin treating the victim with first aid. The emergency alarm (continuous bell) will be sounded. All scouts and staff who are not actively treating the victim must report immediately to the main assembly area.

**Other Potentially Dangerous Critters**  
Also regulars at Camp Lawton are turkeys, rattlesnakes, black widow spiders, and mountain lions (only seen during the winter, but they have been seen). As a general rule, just stay back and observe from a safe distance. If you believe there is any risk at all with the animal, report it to the Camp Director and follow the bear sighting protocols.

### **Fire Safety and Evacuation Procedures**

Wildfires are a reality in this environment, making strict adherence to fire safety and emergency communication protocols essential.

1. **Campfire Regulations \-** All campfires are subject to current Forest Service regulations. When fires are permitted, they must **never** be built above knee level (2 to 3 feet high) and must be monitored closely.  
2. **Fire in Camp (Immediate Threat) \-** If you observe a wildfire or an out-of-control campfire within the camp, **immediately** contact a member of the camp administration or senior staff. The camp alarm will sound so the threat can be evaluated and addressed.   
3. **Distant Fires (Monitoring Phase) \-** If there is a wildfire on the mountain that does not pose an immediate threat to the camp, your job is to remain calm and keep the Scouts relaxed.Do not speculate or spread rumors about what you think is happening. Information will be distributed through official channels only. Spreading rumors during an emergency creates unnecessary panic and will result in disciplinary action.

The absolute first priority for all staff is assisting the Scouts and ensuring they evacuate safely. Retrieving personal gear is a secondary concern and will only be permitted if leadership deems it completely safe to do so.

### **Lightning and Severe Thunderstorms**

1. **The 30-Second Rule (Danger Threshold) \-** Any sign of lightning followed by thunder within less than 30 seconds is considered an immediate danger. When this threshold is reached, all outdoor activities must cease immediately.   
2. **Seeking Shelter**  
   1. **Primary Action:** If you can do so safely, all staff and campers should relocate to the Dining Hall and remain there until the "all clear" is given.  
   2. **Shelter in Place:** If you are already in a safe, secure area and it is unsafe to travel across camp, stay where you are and immediately radio the Camp Director with your location.  
3. **Staff Assignments and Site Checks \-** During a storm, camp administration may need to dispatch staff to check on specific Troop campsites.   
   1. **Do Not Self-Assign:** Never take on this responsibility without direct orders from a supervisor. Ensure your exact whereabouts are known by leadership at all times.  
   2. **Movement Hazards:** If you are assigned to move through camp to check sites, strictly avoid poles, large trees, and open high ground.  
4. **Escalation and Evacuation \-** If lightning strikes dangerously close and an immediate threat to the campsites is assumed, the camp alarm (continuous bell) will sound to trigger a mandatory evacuation of those areas to the Dining Hall. Prior to an alarm sounding, it remains the individual troops' prerogative to seek heavier shelter if they feel their campsite is inadequate.

### **Fatality Protocol**

In the tragic event of a fatality at camp, immediate scene preservation, respect for the deceased, and limiting camper exposure are the absolute priorities.

1. **Immediate Reporting \-** Notify the first available director or senior staff member immediately. They will take charge of the scene and instantly contact the Camp Director and the Health Officer.   
2. **Secure and Shield the Area**  
   1. **Clear Onlookers:** Immediately remove all campers, adult leaders, and unnecessary staff from the vicinity.  
   2. **Cover the Individual:** Staff should respectfully cover the deceased to ensure privacy and minimize trauma to bystanders.  
3. **Preserve the Scene \- Touch nothing and move nothing.** Until authorities arrive, the area must be strictly preserved. Do not disturb the individual, any equipment, personal items, or the surrounding environment.  
4. **Leadership Handover and Support \-** Once senior staff and the Camp Director arrive, they will take exclusive control of the situation, the scene, and all official communications. Because an incident of this nature is highly traumatic, professional counseling and support services will be provided to campers and staff as needed.

Sure it is, but it’s master content, just curated by me. I’ve dug up information from experts and put together what I think is an easy to follow but highly advanced guide to creating genuinely funny skits and songs from scratch.

But imagine if the next great skit, came from YOU\! There’s no reason you can’t write a sketch all by yourself that’s funny, appropriate, and topical. 

### **How To Write Funny**

*“It’s said that a comic is a person who says funny things but a comedian says things funny. This makes me a juggler.” \-Michael Davis* 

Sources: *How To Write Funny* by Scott Dikkers; *Laughing Matters* (video) Starring Rowan Atkinson, written by David Hinton, Robin Driscoll, and Rowan Atkinson; *The Upright Citizens Brigade Comedy Improvisation Manual* by Matt Walsh, Ian Roberts, and Matt Besser

**Module 1: Finding Your Core Concept**

* Generate Ideas Constantly: Make a daily habit of brainstorming at least 10 funny ideas or short jokes to build a stockpile of raw material.  
* The "First Laugh" Test: A good skit starts with a winning concept that can be expressed as a simple 5–10 word title. Test your ideas on a feedback group; if the premise doesn’t get an immediate "First Laugh," it won't work as a full skit.  
* Establish One Impossible Thing: Your skit should introduce a unique comedy world containing only *one* element that is askew from reality. If you introduce too many crazy elements, the audience will get confused.  
* Include Hidden Subtext: Ensure your concept is grounded in an astute observation or opinion about human nature or society (Subtext), but never explicitly state this message to the audience.

**Module 2: Establishing the Scene and the "Take"**

* Build the Base Reality: Before any comedy happens, establish the "Who, What, and Where" of the scene. Ground the scene in a relatable, normal context so the absurd elements have something to contrast against.  
* Find the Game of the Scene (The Take): Identify the "first unusual thing" that breaks the normal pattern of your base reality. This unusual behavior or absurd situation becomes your "take" or the "game"—the single specific idea that makes the skit funny.

**Module 3: Brainstorming and Joke Discipline**

* Ask "If/Then": To generate the comedy for your skit, ask yourself: *"If this unusual thing is true, then what else is true?"*.  
* Riff "Joke Beats": Brainstorm a list of 20 or more joke beats that spool out from your core concept. These beats will form the skeleton of your skit, occurring every few lines.  
* Maintain Joke Discipline: You must ruthlessly cut any jokes that veer away from your core premise, no matter how funny they are on their own. Derailing from the established "track" is a common beginner mistake that confuses audiences.

**Module 4: Crafting Comedic Characters**

* Use Simple Archetypes: Comedic characters are not meant to be deep or realistic; they are two-dimensional representations of human flaws. Rely on proven Character Archetypes (like the Trickster, the Bumbling Authority, or the Dummy) and give them just 1–3 clear traits.  
* Let Traits Drive the Dialogue: Don't write random, clever "gags" for your characters to say. Humor naturally arises simply by having the characters act and speak in strict accordance with their Archetypal traits.  
* Play it Straight: Characters must fully commit to their absurd reality and react truthfully (playing at the "top of their intelligence"). Avoid winking at the audience, acting "wacky," or using silly, cliché character names just for a cheap laugh.

**Module 5: Escalation and Drafting**

* Escalate the Humor: Your skit must build momentum. Every new joke beat should escalate the stakes, heightening the scene to be bigger, crazier, and more absurd than the last.  
* Fold in the Exposition: Do not write long, humorless setups to explain the logic of your scene. Integrate necessary exposition seamlessly into your joke beats.  
* Enter "Clown Mode": When writing your first draft, turn off your inner critic. String your joke beats together quickly and finish the draft without second-guessing or editing yourself.

**Module 6: The Closer and the Edit**

* Cap it with a Button: End the skit with a "closer" or a "button"—a final, definitive joke that wraps up the scene. This is the only place in the skit where you are allowed to slightly break the internal logic or flip the premise upside down for a final surprise.  
* Enter "Editor Mode": Put the draft away for a few days, then return with an objective eye. Cut any needless words to keep the pacing tight.  
* Test and Rewrite: Show the completed draft to your feedback group or test it on a real audience. If the joke beats don't land or the audience gets confused, be prepared to scrap parts of it and rewrite until it works.

### **Writing Songs**

When the Llama Song was written, it began with the 1994 Camp Lawton Cub Scout Camp staff having *Na Na Hey Hey (Kiss Him Goodbye)* (Steam, 1969\) stuck in their heads. That just naturally evolved into “Llama llama \- I ride a \- ride a llama.” Amidst giggles over the brilliance of that wordplay, staff member Morgan Grantham went away for a bit and wrote the lyrics to the verses as a rap which later became the tune to We Will Rock You (Queen, 1977). That's how great songs are written sometimes. It's just a matter of finding the hook.

When you're leading a troop around the campfire or trying to keep energy up on the logs, you don't have the luxury of a backing track or a synthesizer. The song has to be entirely driven by the group's collective energy.  
Here is a cheat sheet for writing a camp song that meets your needs: it demands participation, teaches itself, and sticks in the head long after the fire burns out.

#### 

#### **The Melodic Blueprint**

* **The Staircase Melody:** Keep the core melody restricted to a tight range of notes that move up and down in small, predictable steps. If the notes jump around too frantically, it becomes difficult for an untrained group to sing acapella.  
* **The "Millennial Whoop" Equivalent:** You need a wordless, rhythmic vocalization. The "Woo-oo\!" in DuckTales. Camp songs thrive on these nonsensical, rhythmic syllables (think "Boom Chicka Boom" or "Flea Fly Flow"). They are universally accessible, require zero memorization, and immediately unify the group's timing.

#### 

#### **Structuring for "Zero-Friction" Learning**

*  **The "One at a Time" Rule:** Borrow pop producer Max Martin’s philosophy: never introduce too many elements at once. If the melody is complex, keep the lyrics dead simple. If the lyrics tell a detailed story, keep the melody to a basic chant.  
* **Call and Response (The Echo):** The absolute easiest way to teach a song without accompaniment is the "Repeat After Me" format. You sing a line, the group echoes it. By the time you reach the chorus, they are already trained on the rhythm and key.  
* **The Zipper Structure:** Design the verses so that only *one* *word* changes each time. For example, "We're going on a bear hunt" simply zippers in a new obstacle (mud, river, grass) while the rest of the framework remains identical. This minimizes the cognitive load on the singers.

#### 

#### **Writing Lyrics that Stick**

* **Bookend Rhymes:** Rhyming at both the beginning and the end of phrases makes lyrics significantly more memorable. It gives the brain a predictive puzzle to solve, making the next line feel inevitable.  
* **Action-Oriented Verbs:** Camp songs need kinetic energy. Use words that easily translate to physical movements. If the song mentions rain, there should be a corresponding hand motion. This taps into kinesthetic learning, helping people remember the words through muscle memory.  
* **Escalation:** A great camp song builds tension or absurdity. It should get faster, louder, or fundamentally weirder with each verse until it culminates in a high-energy, chaotic finale.

#### 

#### **Designing the Acapella "Track"**

* **Body Percussion:** Since you have no instruments, the arrangement must include built-in percussion. Dictate specific moments for stomps, claps, or lap-slaps. This serves a dual purpose: it keeps the tempo from dragging (a common problem in acapella group singing) and provides an easy participation avenue for those who might be too shy to sing loudly at first.

* **Be Present:** Arrive on time to all meals, assemblies, and program areas. Sleeping on the job, leaving your assigned area without permission, or abandoning your duties creates a safety hazard and places an unfair burden on your team.  
* **Stay Sharp:** Maintain excellent personal hygiene, adhere to the uniform guidelines at all times, and keep your living quarters and assigned facilities clean and organized.  
* **Respect the Hierarchy:** Follow the instructions of your supervisors and the camp administration. If you disagree with a directive, comply first (unless it is unsafe) and use the open-door grievance policy to discuss it afterward.

**2\. Respect for the Community**

* **Assume Positive Intent:** Treat everyone with the courtesy and kindness outlined in the Scout Law. Abusive language, obscene gestures, bullying, hazing, and malicious pranks have no place at Camp Lawton.  
* **Maintain Boundaries:** Respect the privacy of others. Never enter another cabin without permission, and strictly adhere to all Safeguarding Youth guidelines regarding one-on-one contact and appropriate supervision.  
* **Honor the Environment:** Care for camp equipment and property as if it were your own. Unauthorized use of camp materials or driving vehicles in camp without express permission from the Ranger and Camp Director is prohibited.

**3\. Integrity & Focus**

* **Media Standards:** Any media consumed in camp must align with Scouting values. Staff may not view R-rated movies or play M-rated video games on camp property.  
* **Financial Integrity:** Staff may not accept gratuities, bribes, or tips from campers or visitors, nor may they sell personal items or services on camp property.  
* **Authorized Visitors:** All visitors must be approved in advance by the Camp Director and follow all sign-in procedures to ensure camp security.

### **Zero-Tolerance: Immediate Discharge**

While we prioritize coaching, certain actions pose an immediate threat to the physical, emotional, or legal safety of our campers, staff, and organization. Involvement in any of the following will result in **immediate dismissal**, removal from camp property, and, when applicable, reporting to law enforcement and the Department of Child Safety:

1. **Safety & Abuse Violations:** Any violation of Safeguarding Youth policies, including sexual or physical harassment, abuse, hazing, or any sexual activities on camp property.  
2. **Substance Violations:** The possession, use, or being under the influence of alcohol, illicit drugs, or unprescribed medications. (Smoking or vaping by anyone under 21 is strictly prohibited; authorized use by those 21+ is heavily restricted).  
3. **Theft & Destruction:** Stealing personal or camp property, gambling, or willful destruction/vandalism of camp facilities.  
4. **Contraband:** Possession or distribution of pornography, firearms, or any items listed on the "Strictly Prohibited" list.  
5. **Falsification:** Misrepresenting information on your application, medical forms, or official incident reports.  
6. **Endangerment:** Transporting a minor in a vehicle without written parental permission filed directly with the Camp Director.
